About the Project

The exhibition From the Inside Out aims to show the "insides" of trees, the tissues that make up the trunk and branches, and the way in which the tissues are organised into different patterns, depending on the species. In addition to their undeniable aesthetic value, the anatomical images also reveal their function in the tree.

The exhibition has two components: a physical one and a digital one.

The physical aspect consists of an exhibition of 15 panels displayed in the Coimbra University Botanical Garden, which will be available until 25 June 2023. With this exhibition, besides being possible to learn about each of the 15 tree species and admire images of their anatomy, the visitor has the opportunity to tour the Coimbra Botanical Garden, getting to know it from a different perspective.

The physical exhibition will be continued in the digital exhibition. Through the digital exhibition it will be possible to know "inside out" a total of 38 species from the Botanical Garden of the University of Coimbra. The digital component allows the use of interactivity, animations and visualization of information that create a different experience for the user, in which they can learn all the information displayed in a simple and clear way.

About the Anatomy +

Wood is part of our everyday objects, the chairs we sit on, the tables we work at, the doors that open and close, the roof structures, the warmth of the fireplace. For a tree, wood is its mechanical support, which allows it to reach great heights and to support its crown. But it is also through wood that water is transported from the roots to the leaves, to hydrate the tissues. The whole wood is made of parts, which are the cells organised in tissues, each one with its own function, the fascinating world of wood anatomy.

Anatomy and Function

Most of the trunk and branches are made up of xylem, commonly known as wood. The wood is produced by a small layer of cells, the vascular cambium, which divides every year, leading to an increase in the perimeter of the trunk and the formation of growth rings.

The main function of the xylem is to transport water from the roots to the leaves and to provide mechanical support. It is made up mostly of dead cells, but also has some living parenchyma cells organised in rays, with the function of storing nutrients and water and repairing wounds in the wood. The ability to lift water to incredible heights reveals that trees are true hydraulic engineers. To lift water from the roots to the leaves, through the dead cells, the tree uses the driving force of water evaporation that occurs in the leaves. The tissue that surrounds the xylem and vascular cambium is called phloem, and is essentially made up of living cells, with the function of transporting sugars from the leaves to the roots.

Anatomy and Evolution

Anatomy also tells stories about the evolution of plants. Through wood anatomy it is possible to distinguish two large groups of plants, the Gymnosperms, plants that present bare seeds, which are older in the evolutionary history, and the Angiosperms, plants with flowers and seeds protected by the fruit, which are more recent. The wood of the Gymnosperms is fundamentally constituted by a single type of cell, the tracheid, which assumes the double function of water and mechanical transport. In Angiosperms we can find two types of cells, vessels specialised in water transport and fibres with a mechanical support function.

About the Process +

To obtain the wood anatomy images, samples go through a series of processes, the final result of which allows the visualisation of tissues and cells using an optical microscope.

1º Sample Collection

We start by cutting a small woody branch and, in the laboratory, a small cylinder is sectioned from this branch and then placed in histological boxes. In these boxes the identification of the sample is written with a pencil, which can be the name of the species or a code defined by the collector of the samples.

Caption: Histological boxes.

2º Fixation, Dehydration, Clearing, Infiltration

The tissues now undergo a series of processes, from the interruption of metabolic processes (fixation), removal of water (dehydration), preparation for paraffin impregnation (bleaching) and paraffin infiltration.

The histological boxes are then placed in a fixative liquid, FAA (formaldehyde and acetic acid), which aims to stop all the metabolic reactions of the cells, namely the activity of enzymes, which could degrade the tissues.

Afterwards, the tissues undergo a gradual dehydration, being immersed in an increasing sequence of ethanol concentrations. If we initially used a high concentration of ethanol, tissue dehydration could be so fast that it would cause tissue deformation. The aim is to visualize the tissues as close to their shape as if they were alive.

In this step, free water from the tissues is removed, which is essential for the next steps, which involve the impregnation of the tissues with a hydrophobic compound, that is, it repels water. If the tissues were filled with water, it would not be possible to impregnate the tissues with the hydrophobic compound.

After going through the dehydration process, the tissues are put in a compound called “Bio-Clear” that will remove the ethanol from the dehydration process. The tissues acquire a transparent appearance and for this reason this step is called clearing.

The tissues are now dipped in liquid paraffin at a temperature of 60˚C, a step called infiltration. The liquid paraffin infiltrates the tissues and, when placed at room temperature, becomes solid, stiffening the tissues. This step is fundamental to obtain thin sections of the tissues.

3º Paraffin Blocks Production

The paraffin infiltrated samples are now placed on a paraffin block. The goal is to place the tissue in the orientation in which we intend to make the cut. The orientation can be transverse, radial or tangential.

For this step, an equipment called a paraffin table is used. The paraffin table consists of three main parts.

In one of the parts, we store the histological boxes with the samples infiltrated with paraffin, and the paraffin moulds, small metallic trays. This part is maintained at a temperature of 25-30˚C. The other part consists of the paraffin tank, heated to 60°C to keep the liquid paraffin, and the paraffin dispenser, to fill the metal moulds with liquid paraffin. The third part of the paraffin table is an ice plate that quickly solidifies the liquid paraffin.

The fabric is placed in the metal mould and some liquid paraffin is added. The fabric is oriented in the desired position, and more paraffin is added to form a block. It is then placed on the ice sheet for the paraffin to solidify quickly and keep the fabric in the correct position.

4º Cutting the Paraffin Blocks

For observation under an optical microscope, it is necessary to obtain very thin cuts so that light can pass through the tissues. Only in this way is it possible to observe the details of tissues and cells. This is done using a piece of equipment called a microtome.

The paraffin block with the tissue is cut in an equipment called microtome, to obtain thin cuts, between 7 and 10 µm thick (1 µm is 0.001 mm). The cuts obtained in the microtome are placed in a container with tepid water, where they float. Then, a glass slide previously impregnated with glycerine is used to glue the tissues to the slide.

5º Coloration

To better observe the tissues, they must be stained. There is a large diversity of histological dyes that can be applied to tissues with the sole aim of contrasting them, or with the aim of marking certain structures.

For the dyes to infiltrate the tissues, it is now necessary to remove the paraffin. In addition, most dyes are hydrophilic, that is, they need water to impregnate the tissues. Therefore, the tissue needs to be hydrated again. Thus, in this phase, the slides with the tissue are placed at 60˚C to make the paraffin liquid and facilitate its removal, then they are placed in “Bio Clear”, followed by a decreasing phase of ethanol solutions until reaching a solution with water to hydrate the tissues. The slides are then placed in the dye, or dye mixture. Excess dye is then washed off with water. For a long-term preservation of the stained tissues, the samples must be dehydrated again. In the last phase, when a lamella is applied to protect the tissues, a drop of resin, Canada balsam, is placed, which will seal the sample, that is, it removes the tissue from contact with air, allowing it to remain unaltered for long periods of time.

Curiosity +

In the samples from the INSIDE OUT exhibition, two dyes were used, safranin, a red dye, and Astra blue, a blue dye. Astra Blue stains cell walls made of cellulose with a blue colour. Safranin stains cell walls with a red colour, which, in addition to cellulose, contain a compound called lignin. Lignin is a compound that is very resistant to degradation and, in addition, it is hydrophobic. Cells whose walls become impregnated with lignin eventually die, leaving only the cell walls. Therefore, these two dyes make it possible to distinguish walls that are lignified (red) from those that are not lignified (blue).
